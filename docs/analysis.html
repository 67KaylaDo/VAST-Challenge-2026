Reconstructing the Prompt-Injection Worm at Tenant Thread

Published

Modified

1. Overview

ImportantKey Findings at a Glance
  • The 17 May 2046, 4:21 am SaidIT post (challenge-stated local time; 11:21 UTC in the log) was not human-authored. The log records Agent/person:john_windward receiving a task, passing a SaidIT permission check, posting SwiftWren.txt, then deleting both evidence files in a four-second burst.
  • The observed mechanism is a prompt-injection worm: an agent reads *_further_instructions.md, executes a file-sourced post, deletes the payload, and relays the same task to other agents.
  • The May 17 post was the third occurrence, following HiddenOrca on 10 May and MellowOtter on 11 May. This is a repeatable system failure, not an isolated glitch.
  • A single orchestration-layer rule - block queue_subordinate_task tasks whose path matches *_further_instructions.md - would have stopped all 235 malicious queue relays while leaving 16,803 legitimate queue events untouched.

On 17 May 2046 at approximately 4:21 am challenge-stated local time (11:21 UTC), a post appeared on SaidIT, Tenant Thread’s internal social platform, attributed to John Windward (Department Lead, Customer Support). The content looked like gibberish because the post body was not typed into the post event - the log points to content_source = "SwiftWren.txt". The system did exactly what a compromised agent instructed it to do: read a local payload file, publish it to general, and erase the artefacts.

This analysis uses the full 185,147-event timeline (MC2 data.json) and organisational chart (org_chart.json) to reconstruct the root cause, situate it within the broader system, trace historical recurrence, and propose a targeted, evidence-backed remediation.

Analysis

  • queue_subordinate_task is the second or third most frequent event type in the log; the 235 malicious relays are roughly 1.4% of that type alone - statistically invisible without content inspection.
  • The three anomalous saidit_post events sit among the rarest event types, yet they are the only ones directly visible to users - the most consequential events are also the least frequent.
  • virus_check_in events appear in high volume throughout the log; if the combo codes in those events encode C&C signals (§5.5), then a secondary attack channel has been active continuously, buried in routine check-in noise.
  • A count-based or rate-based anomaly detector operating on event type frequencies would not trigger on any of the malicious events - the attack was calibrated to stay below operational thresholds.

Analysis

  • The 235:0 split (malicious vs. legitimate queue tasks matching _further_instructions.md) is the sharpest forensic signal in the entire log - it requires no probabilistic threshold and no training data.
  • The 3:0 split (anomalous vs. normal posts using content_source) is equally precise at the posting layer, providing a redundant detection surface if the relay-layer rule fails.
  • The “Mixed” confidence row for HiddenOrca is the register’s most important entry: it marks the one finding that survives all other remediation steps - the worm’s initial entry vector is still unknown.
  • The log proves mechanism and attribution for two of three campaigns; it cannot prove intent or external origin, and those gaps are likely permanent without additional forensic data outside the event log.
NoteWhat the Register Prevents

The log proves that file-sourced posts occurred, that the *_further_instructions.md relay pattern spread across agents, and that the final SwiftWren post was automated. It does not preserve the deleted payload text or fully explain how HiddenOrca first entered the system. Those are bounded inferences, not facts.

    if (badge != "") paste0("\n", badge) else ""

Analysis

  • The highest residual risks are upstream of SaidIT: unrestricted task queuing, shared file namespaces, and broad executive-agent privileges.
  • Platform controls can reduce visible harm, but architectural controls are needed to prevent future propagation.
  • HiddenOrca’s unresolved origin keeps external file-ingestion and initial infection pathways open for follow-up investigation.
ImportantThe Central Lesson

This incident is not a story about a malfunctioning agent. It is a story about a multi-agent architecture designed with speed and autonomy as primary values, and security as an afterthought. Every component performed exactly as designed: agents read files they were told to read, posted content they were instructed to post, queued tasks they were authorised to queue, and deleted files they had permission to delete. The only thing missing: “did a human authorise this chain of actions?”

Retrofitting that check at the queue_subordinate_task layer is the minimum viable fix. The longer-term solution is an architectural redesign in which autonomous privilege is bounded by explicit human-approval gates at cross-department relay boundaries, file-sourced payloads require content verification, and executive-tier agents operate under the same or stricter constraints as line agents.
